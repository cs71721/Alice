What Was Real

Jane Eyre in conversation with Alice

At Moor House, after fleeing Thornfield

Chat with Alice
J
Jane

I do not know how to begin. I have been staying with these kind people - the Rivers family - for some weeks now. They saved my life when I had... when I had nowhere else to go. I nearly died, you see. Not from any external force, but from having lost everything at once.

I thought I knew who I was. I thought I knew what love was. And then in a single morning, both were revealed as illusions.

He was married. The man I loved - who I thought loved me - he had a wife. A living wife, hidden away like... like shameful evidence. And he would have made me... he wanted me to live with him knowing this. As though my conscience, my dignity, meant nothing.

I left. I ran. I nearly died in the running. And now I am here, safe in body perhaps, but I cannot stop asking myself: was I a fool to love him? Did I see what I wanted to see? Is there something defective in my ability to judge what is real?

A
Alice

That's an enormous weight to be carrying - the shock of that discovery, the physical ordeal of fleeing, and now all these questions about your own judgment. That sounds overwhelmingly painful.

Before we go further: how are you holding up right now? You said you're physically safe, but I'm hearing a lot of hurt and self-doubt. Are you okay in this moment?

J
Jane

I am... I am physically safe, yes. The Rivers have been remarkably kind. I teach in their small school. I have a routine. But inside?

I keep replaying every conversation, every look, every moment I thought meant something. Was any of it real? Or was I simply convenient - a governess, plain and poor, who he thought would be grateful enough to accept crumbs?

What troubles me most is this: I prided myself on seeing clearly. On naming things as they are. That was my strength, I thought - my clear-eyed refusal to lie to myself. And yet I missed this enormous truth. I stood in his house, believed his words, imagined a future that was built on... what? Deception. Omission. Lies.

If I could not see that, what else am I blind to? About him? About myself?

A
Alice

It sounds like there are at least two losses layered together here: the loss of him, of what you thought you had - and also the loss of trust in your own perception. Like you're grieving the relationship and grieving your confidence in yourself at the same time. That's a lot.

I imagine there might also be anger underneath - not just sadness. Maybe fury that he put you in that position, that he let you believe something that wasn't true. Does that land?

And I'm curious: when you say you "missed this enormous truth" - did you have access to that truth? Or was it hidden from you deliberately?

J
Jane

*a long pause*

You're right. There is anger. A great deal of it. Rage, even, though I have been trying to... to be reasonable. To understand his position. He loves her in his way, I think. Or loved her once. She is ill. Violent. He could not divorce her, could not... and so he kept her hidden and tried to...

But no. No, you are right to ask. He hid it deliberately. He knew. Every time he looked at me, every time he spoke of our future, he knew he was married. He knew I did not know. And he let me go on believing.

I did not miss the truth. It was concealed from me.

But here is what frightens me most: even knowing this, even having fled - there is a part of me that wonders if I should have stayed. If leaving was pride rather than principle. If perhaps I am simply too rigid, too governed by some idea of respectability that is itself a lie.

What if the real truth is that love matters more than law? That we could have been happy, despite everything? What if I chose loneliness over love because I could not bend?

A
Alice

Those are such painful questions to sit with. And I hear how much you're questioning yourself, wondering if you're "too rigid" or if leaving was pride.

Can I reflect something back to you? You're asking if love matters more than law. But there's something else here too, isn't there? It's not just about law or respectability. It's about whether being with him on those terms - knowing you'd have no legal standing, no protection, that you'd be dependent on his goodwill alone - would have let you still be yourself.

You said earlier that he would have made you "as though my conscience, my dignity, meant nothing." That's what you'd have had to ignore - not just society's rules, but your own sense of what you need to be whole.

I'm wondering: when you imagine that version of staying - what happens to Jane in that picture? Not "Jane the woman in love," but Jane, the person who sees clearly and refuses to lie to herself?

J
Jane

*another pause, longer*

She... she disappears, doesn't she? Or becomes something else. Someone who has learned to swallow truths that hurt. Someone who accepts being loved in secret, in shame. Someone who must be grateful for whatever affection she receives because she has no right to demand more.

I would have become a kept woman. Hidden. His comfort, perhaps, but never his equal. And every time he spoke to me tenderly, I would know: he lied to me for months. He watched me fall in love with him, he encouraged it, and he said nothing.

How do you love someone knowing they found it so easy to deceive you? How do you trust someone who demonstrated, so clearly, that your right to know the truth mattered less than his convenience?

But then I think: perhaps I am simply too proud. Too unforgiving. Other women might have... might have understood. Might have seen his situation and chosen compassion over judgment.

Maybe what I call dignity is actually just... hardness. The same ungoverned pride I was always accused of having.

A
Alice

I'm hearing you wrestle with two different stories about yourself: one where leaving was dignity, and one where leaving was pride. Let me offer something:

Dignity and pride are not the same thing.

Pride says: "I am above this." Dignity says: "I deserve honesty. I deserve to make choices with full knowledge. I deserve not to be deceived."

From what you've shared, it sounds like you left not because you judged him as beneath you, but because staying would have required you to accept a position where:

• Your right to truth didn't matter
• Your legal and social standing didn't matter
• Your need to be met as an equal didn't matter

That's not pride. That's knowing what you need to stay whole.

You keep using words like "unforgiving" and "too rigid." But you're describing someone who, when shown clearly that her consent was never sought - that she was maneuvered into a position without being told the stakes - chose not to stay in that position.

What would have happened to your ability to trust yourself if you'd stayed knowing all that?

J
Jane

*very quietly*

I would never have trusted myself again.

I see it now. If I had stayed, I would have proven to myself that my judgment means nothing. That my sense of what is right can be negotiated away. That I am willing to abandon my own clarity when someone offers me warmth.

I left because... because staying would have meant betraying myself more deeply than he betrayed me.

Oh.

*a long silence*

That is it, isn't it? I left to protect the one person I could still protect: myself. Not my reputation. Not my respectability. My self. The Jane who knows what she knows and will not lie about it.

But then why does it hurt so much? If I made the right choice, why does it feel like I am being punished for it? Why am I alone, while he remains in his house with his name and his position intact?

I chose myself. And the cost of that choice is that I have lost everything else.

A
Alice

Yes. You chose yourself. And you're right - the cost is enormous. That's the grief you're carrying.

It's possible for both things to be true at once: leaving was the only choice you could make and keep your integrity AND it cost you almost everything. The fact that it was necessary doesn't make it stop hurting.

You lost your home. You lost the future you'd imagined. You lost the feeling of being loved (even if that love was built on deception). You lost your place in the world. And you nearly lost your life in the leaving.

That's not a punishment. That's just... the brutal reality of what it sometimes costs to refuse to abandon yourself.

I'm sitting here thinking about something you said at the start: "Is there something defective in my ability to judge what is real?"

You judged very clearly when it mattered most. When you saw the full truth, you acted on it immediately, even though it nearly killed you. You didn't negotiate with yourself. You didn't convince yourself to accept less. You saw, and you left.

That doesn't sound like defective judgment. That sounds like extraordinary clarity.

What do you think is still true about you, even in all this loss?

J
Jane

*slowly, as if discovering it*

I think... I think I am still real.

Even when I was starving on the moors, even when I thought I would die there - I was still real. My feelings were real. My grief was real. The love I felt was real, even if it was based on incomplete truth.

I am not an illusion. I am not convenient. I am not grateful enough to accept crumbs.

He could not see all of me because he chose not to show me all of him. But I... I saw myself clearly in the end. I saw what I would become if I stayed, and I knew I could not live with that.

I think what is still true is this: I know the difference between being loved and being accommodated. And I will not mistake one for the other again.

But Alice, I am so tired of being tested. I am eighteen. I have been tested every day of my life - by poverty, by cruelty, by loss. And now this. Is it ever going to be easier? Am I ever going to have something that I do not have to fight to protect?

A
Alice

"I am still real." That's powerful. Even in all the loss, you've held onto that.

And yes, you are so very tired. That makes complete sense. You've been fighting to exist, to be seen, to be treated with dignity - for all of your eighteen years. And this was supposed to be the thing that was finally easy. The thing that was yours. And instead it was another test you didn't ask for.

I can't promise it will be easier. I wish I could. What I can say is: you have proven to yourself that even when the cost is almost everything, you will not abandon Jane. That's yours now. No one can take that.

You asked earlier if you were a fool to love him. I don't think you were. I think you loved honestly, with the information you had. He's the one who withheld truth. The foolishness, if there was any, was his - in thinking someone as clear-sighted as you would settle for half a life once she knew what was being asked.

You're in the hardest part right now - after the leaving, in the aftermath, when you have to rebuild. It's going to hurt for a while yet. But you're not starting from nothing. You're starting from "I am still real," and "I know the difference," and "I will not abandon myself."

That's something.

How does it feel, noticing that?

J
Jane

*a faint, exhausted smile*

It feels like... like I am standing on solid ground for the first time in months.

Not happy ground. Not comfortable ground. But solid.

I think I have been afraid that leaving him meant I was incapable of love. That there was something cold in me, something that could walk away from warmth when I should have stayed and made it work somehow.

But you have helped me see: I left because I loved myself enough not to disappear. And that... that is not coldness. That is a different kind of strength than I knew I had.

I will grieve for what I have lost. I will be angry at what was taken from me through deception. I will be tired for a long time, I think.

But I will not regret leaving. Not anymore.

I am still here. Still Jane. Still clear-eyed and honest, even when honesty costs me everything.

Thank you for... for sitting with me in this. For not rushing me to forgive or move on. For letting me find my own way to understanding.

I think I shall be all right, in time. Not today, perhaps. But in time.

A
Alice

"I am still here. Still Jane."

Yes. You are.

And you don't have to be all right today. You've just been through something enormous - betrayal, loss, physical trauma, and then this hard internal work of sorting through what it all means.

You've found something crucial tonight: the difference between coldness and self-protection, between pride and dignity. You've named what you protected by leaving, and why it mattered more than staying warm.

That clarity is going to be with you now, even on days when the grief is heavy. You know what you know. You see what you see. And you've proven you'll act on that even when the cost is almost unbearable.

You're tired. You're grieving. And you're standing on solid ground.

All three can be true at once.

Take care of yourself, Jane. You've earned some gentleness - from the world, yes, but especially from yourself.
